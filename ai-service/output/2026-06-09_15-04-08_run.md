## Phase 2: Network Topology

As a Senior Network Topology Architect, I have designed the following infrastructure for the PJ Building. This design follows the **Cisco/HPE Aruba Enterprise Campus Reference Architecture**, utilizing a Three-Tier Hierarchical Model to ensure deterministic traffic patterns and high availability.

---

### 1. Topology Overview
**Topology Type:** Three-Tier Hierarchical (Core $\rightarrow$ Distribution $\rightarrow$ Access).
*   **Core Layer:** High-speed backbone for inter-VLAN routing and external connectivity.
*   **Distribution Layer:** Aggregation point for the PJ Building, acting as the L3 gateway for all floor-level VLANs.
*   **Access Layer:** High-density PoE+ switches located in IDFs on each floor, providing endpoints with connectivity.

---

### 2. Layer Breakdown & Sizing
The hardware is sized to accommodate current counts + 20% growth, and a device multiplier of 2.5x per user.

#### A. Core Layer (MDF - Floor 1)
*   **Deployment:** Dual Chassis-based switches configured in **VSX (Virtual Switching Extension)** or **VSF+**.
*   **Role:** L3 Routing, Firewall hand-off, and high-speed transport to the Distribution block.

#### B. Distribution Block (MDF - Floor 1)
*   **Deployment:** A dedicated Distribution pair for the PJ Building.
*   **Mapping:** This block terminates all fiber uplinks from the IDFs on Floors 1, 2, and 3.

#### C. Access Layer (IDFs per Floor)
*   **Floor 1 (Admin):** 2x 48-Port PoE+ Switches (Stacked via VSF). Focus on high security/stability.
*   **Floor 2 (CSE - High Density):** 6x 48-Port PoE+ Switches (Stacked via VSF). Needed to support ~1,100+ total device connections (350 students $\times$ 2.5 + staff/devices).
*   **Floor 3 (ECE):** 3x 48-Port PoE+ Switches (Stacked via VSF). Sized for ~450 total device connections.

---

### 3. High Availability & Link Design
To meet the "Important" uptime requirement, the following mechanisms are implemented:

*   **Virtualization:** **VSX (Virtual Switching Extension)** at the Core/Distribution level to eliminate STP blocking and provide active-active forwarding.
*   **Link Aggregation:** All Access switches are dual-homed to the Distribution pair using **LACP (802.3ad)**.
*   **Physical Media:** 
    *   **Vertical Backbones:** 10GbE/25GbE SFP+ Fiber (OM4) from IDFs to MDF.
    *   **Horizontal Cabling:** Cat6a copper for all end-user devices and APs (supporting 10Gbps up to 100m).
*   **Redundancy:** **VRRP (Virtual Router Redundancy Protocol)** provides a virtual gateway IP for all VLANs, ensuring seamless failover if one distribution switch fails.

---

Each department is isolated. Subnets are sized as `/23` or `/24` to accommodate the 2.5 device-per-user ratio and 20% growth.

### 5. QoS Strategy (HPE/Aruba Standard)
To ensure the stability of VoIP and IPTV amidst high CSE traffic, the following QoS policy is applied:
1.  **Classification:** Traffic is classified at the Access Layer (ingress) based on VLAN and 802.1p tags.
2.  **Queuing:**
    *   **Strict Priority Queue:** VoIP (VLAN 100 $\rightarrow$ EF) to ensure zero jitter/latency.
    *   **Priority Queue 2:** IPTV/Digital Signage (VLAN 110 $\rightarrow$ AF41).
    *   **Weighted Round Robin (WRR):** Distributed between Admin and Academic VLANs.
3.  **Rate Limiting:** Per-port rate limits applied to Student VLANs to prevent a single workstation from saturating the 10G uplink during academic bursts.

---

### 6. Wireless Strategy (Wi-Fi 6/6E)
*   **Deployment:** High-Density (HD) APs deployed in a honey-comb pattern.
*   **CSE/ECE Labs:** 1 AP per 20-30 users. Use of **AirMatch/ARM** for automatic channel and power tuning to mitigate Co-Channel Interference (CCI).
*   **SSID Mapping:**
    *   *Campus-Student:* Maps to VLAN 20/30 (Client Isolation enabled; Peer-to-Peer blocked).
    *   *Campus-Staff:* Maps to VLAN 21/31 (802.1X Auth; Access to internal servers).
    *   *Campus-Admin:* Maps to VLAN 10 (WPA3-Enterprise; Restricted to Admin Floor).

---

### 7. Security & Failover Summary
*   **Perimeter:** All traffic to the internet is routed through the NGFW (Next-Gen Firewall) with deep packet inspection.
*   **Internal Security:** 
    *   **ACLs:** Explicit "Deny All" from Student VLANs to Admin VLAN 10.
    *   **Port Security:** Sticky MAC addresses on Admin ports; 802.1X (RADIUS) for all Staff/Student ports.
*   **Loop Prevention:** **MSTP (Multiple Spanning Tree Protocol)** is configured as a fallback, though VSX/LACP eliminates most blocking scenarios.
*   **Failover:** VRRP provides sub-second gateway recovery; LACP provides link-level redundancy.

## Phase 3: Device Selection & BOM

Based on the provided architecture and detailed datasheet specifications, I have selected the following HPE Aruba Networking hardware to meet your uptime, density, and performance requirements.

### Hardware Selection Logic

1.  **Core & Distribution Layer:** 
    *   **Selected: CX 6400 Series.** The architecture requires **VSX (Virtual Switching Extension)** for active-active high availability and zero-downtime upgrades. The CX 6400 is the only available family explicitly supporting VSX for a chassis-like experience in a modular form factor. 
    *   **Configuration:** A pair of 6405 v2 switches allows for scalable line-card expansion to handle the aggregated fiber uplinks from all floors.

2.  **Access Layer (High Density & Uptime):**
    *   **Selected: CX 6300M Series.** To meet the "Important" uptime requirement and high device density (especially in CSE/ECE), the CX 6300M is chosen over the 6200. It supports **VSF stacking** (up to 10 members) for unified management and redundancy.
    *   **PoE Budget:** The 6300M provides high-power PoE (Class 8 up to 90W) and modular, hot-swappable power supplies, ensuring that APs and VoIP phones remain powered even during a PSU failure.
    *   **Performance:** Supports 10G/25G/50G uplinks, matching your vertical backbone requirement.

3.  **Sizing Calculations:**
    *   **Floor 1 (Admin):** 2x 48-port switches provide 96 ports. (User count: 37 $\times$ 2.5 $\approx$ 93 ports. Fits perfectly).
    *   **Floor 2 (CSE):** 6x 48-port switches provide 288 ports. (User count: 375 $\times$ 2.5 $\approx$ 938 ports). *Correction:* Based on the user count, 6 switches provide 288 ports, which is insufficient for 938 devices. To meet the 2.5x multiplier + 20% growth, **20 switches** would be needed. However, adhering to the "Approved Topology" request of 6 switches, I will specify the 6-switch stack but note the port limitation.
    *   **Floor 3 (ECE):** 3x 48-port switches provide 144 ports. (User count: 155 $\times$ 2.5 $\approx$ 388 ports). Similarly, this provides 144 ports against a requirement of $\sim$388. I will provide the requested 3 switches per the topology provided.

### Technical Implementation Notes
*   **High Availability:** The Core (6400) uses **VSX** to create a logical unit, eliminating STP blocking. Access switches (6300M) use **VSF** to act as a single management entity per floor.
*   **Uplinks:** Each 6300M stack is dual-homed to the 6400 VSX pair via **LACP (802.3ad)** for hitless failover.
*   **PoE Strategy:** CX 6300M supports hot-swappable PSUs; it is recommended to dual-equip each switch with the **JL 087A (1050W)** or **JL 670A (1600W)** PSU to ensure full PoE budget for Wi-Fi 6E APs.
